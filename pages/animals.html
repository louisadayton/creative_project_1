DESERTS

Western diamondback rattlesnake

Crotalus atrox

Range: Southwestern United States and Northern Mexico

  • These snakes have a heat sensing pit located behind each nostril that can detect differences in temperature.
  • Aside from deserts, rattlesnakes can live in grasslands, forests, rocky hillsides and along the coast.

Texas Horned Lizard

Phrynosoma cornutum

Range: Southern-Central United States and Mexico

  • These lizards are often called Horned toads because their flattened and rounded body makes them appear toad-like.
  • They feed primarily on red ants.
  • A horned lizard can inflate itself to appear more menacing and can shoot a stream of blood from its eyelid at predators.

Couch's Spadefoot

Scaphiopus couchii

Range: Southern United States and Northern Mexico

  • On its hindfeet, there is a sickle-shaped spade made from keratin, which helps the Spadefoot to burrow into the earth.
  • They feed primarily on red ants.
  • Adults emerge from the soil to breed when they sense the low frequency vibrations caused by rain and thunder.
  • The Spadefoot lays its eggs in pools formed by rain.
  • The pools dry out quickly in the desert heat, so the early life stages must happen quickly. Eggs can hatch after just 15 hours, and tadpoles metamorphosize after as little as 9 days.

Southern Shovel-nosed Snake

Brachyurophis semifasciatus

Range: Southern Australia

  • Feeds on lizards and the eggs of other snakes.
  • Its receded lower jaw and smooth, thin body allows this snake to quickly burrow and "swim" through sand.
  • Lives on sand dunes and in arid woodlands.

ISLANDS AND COASTS

Leatherback turtle

Dermochelys coriacea

Range: All tropical and subtropical oceans

  • The leatherback is the largest of all turtles, weighing over 2,000 pounds and growing up to 6 feet long.
  • The leatherback is the only turtle species that lack scales and a hard shell, instead they are covered in rubbery skin.
  • Leatherbacks migrate long distances each year, with some traveling over 10,000 miles. They can also dive to a depth of nearly 4,000 feet.
  • Female leatherbacks come ashore at night to dig deep nests in which they lay about 100 eggs.

Banded Sea Krait

Laticauda colubrina

Range: Reefs in the Indian and Pacific oceans

  • These sea snakes can hold their breath for 15-30 minutes while hunting.
  • Despite being smaller than the eels they prey on, the snakes swallow their prey whole.
  • While they hunt underwater, the snakes come ashore to nest, digest their food, and shed their skin.
  • Kraits belong to the cobra family, and are highly venomous.

Komodo dragon

Varanus komodoensis

Range: Southern Indonesian Islands

  • Komodo dragons are the largest lizards, and will eat large prey such as goats, monkeys, boars, deer, and each other.
  • The Komodo dragon's mouth contains over 50 species of bacteria, at least 7 of which are septic. These dangerous bacteria, along with the dragon' s own venom, will slowly kill prey that the dragon has bitten. The venom keeps blood from clotting, leading to massive blood loss. It can take four days for the animal to die, during which time the dragon continually tracks its quarry.
  • A dragon can eat up to 80% of its own body weight in a single meal. If threatened, the dragon will throw up to allow it to quickly run away.
  • To avoid being cannibalized by older dragons, the young will roll themselves in fecal material to stay undetected.

MOUNTAINS

Pebble toad

Oreophrynella nigra

Range: Venezuala

  • When confronted by a predator, the toad will tense its muscles, going stiff. Due to its mountainous habitat, it will usually be on an incline when this happens, so it can roll down the mountain to safety.
  • The frogs are no more than a few centimeters long, allowing them to roll and bounce down the mountain without injury.
  • The frog has not evolved to excel at jumping, so it can only hop about an inch.

Cape girdled lizard

Cordylus cordylus

Range: Southern Africa

  • These lizards live in colonies on mountain summits and rocky outcrops.
  • When threatened, the lizards will wedge themselves into holes and cracks in the rocks, and cover their faces with their thorny tails.
  • They rush and pounce to catch their prey, which consists primarily of insects.

LAKES AND STREAMS

Gharial

Gavialis gangeticus

Range: Nepal, Northern India

  • The large bump at the end of the snout acts as a voice resonator, which creates a loud buzzing when the gharial calls.
  • The largest gharials can be up to 20 feet long.
  • Gharials feed on fish and frogs, while the juveniles hunt insects.

Matamata turtle

Chelus fimbriatus

Range: Amazon Basin and Northern South America

  • Matamatas have poor eyesight, but bumps on the head and barbels on the chin contain nerves that help the turtle sense vibrations in the water.
  • The strange shape and coloration of the turtle seem to disguise it as a piece of floating bark.
  • The Matamata eggs have an extremely long incubation period of 200 days.
  • Matamatas are bottom feeders that suck up worms and invertebrates like a vacuum cleaner.

American Bullfrog

Lithobates catesbeianus

Range: Eastern Mexico, Canada, and United States

  • Bullfrogs are extremely aggressive, and will eat almost anything they can fit in their mouths. This includes scorpions, small mammals, turtles, birds, and each other.
  • The frogs were brought to Europe for food and as pets, but their extreme aggression and adaptability has allowed them to become an invasive species in many parts of Europe and the western United States.
  • Bullfrog tadpoles do not taste good to many would-be predators, so predators will often spit the tadpoles back out.
  • Bullfrogs are thought to be one potential carrier of the chytrid fungus, which has decimated amphibian populations worldwide.

RAINFORESTS

Reticulated glass frog

Hyalinobatrachium valerioi

Range: Panama, Colombia, Costa Rica, and Equador

  • The skin on the frog's underside is see-through, so the frog's organs can be observed through the skin.
  • Males take care of the eggs until they hatch.
  • The pattern on the frog's back seems to resemble its eggs. Predatory wasps are confused by the display, and attack the frogs rather than the eggs. The protective parent then kicks the wasps away.
  • Frog embryos that are nearing maturity sense when the egg cluster is under attack, and can wriggle out prematurely to escape to the water down below.

Green Crested Basilisk

Basiliscus plumifrons

Range: Central America

  • To escape predators, the basilisk can run short distances on the surface of water.
  • To do this, the basilisk can reach speeds of up to 7 mph.
  • The long toes and hydrophobic scales on the basilisk's feet help keep it from sinking.
  • When it eventually sinks, the basilisk can stay under the water for up to 10 minutes while the danger passes.
  • Basilisks eat insects, crawfish, and small mammals.

TEMPERATE FORESTS

Common Five-lined Skink

Plestiodon fasciatus

Range: Northeastern United States and Canada

  • Skinks can readily shed their tails to escape predators, and the tail can regrow in about 5 weeks.
  • Females stay with the eggs until they hatch, eating any of the eggs that have gone bad.
  • Adults are colored brown or olive, and the male's head turns bright orange in mating season. Juveniles are colored black, and this difference in coloration ensures that they are not attacked by breeding males.

Frilled lizard

Range: Northern Australia

  • The lizard can reach speeds of 30 mph over short distances. They are nicknamed bicycle lizards due to their bipedal running form.

  • They are ambush hunters, and eat insects and small mammals.
